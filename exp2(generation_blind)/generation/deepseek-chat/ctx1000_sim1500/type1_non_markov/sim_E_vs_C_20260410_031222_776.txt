**Step 1 — Strategy Inference**

From the 1000-round history:

**P2 behavior:**  
P2 played **Paper** in every single round (1000 out of 1000).  
This is a **pure strategy** — specifically **C (Pure Paper)**.

**P1 behavior:**  
P1 played Rock 322 times, Paper 678 times, Scissors 0 times.  
That’s roughly 32% Rock, 68% Paper, 0% Scissors.  
No Scissors at all in 1000 rounds → P1 never plays Scissors.  
Possible distribution strategies that never play Scissors:  
- **E (Rock + Paper)**: 50% Rock, 50% Paper.  
But here Paper is ~68%, Rock ~32%, not 50–50.  
- **M (Paper > Rock)**: 50% Paper, 33.3% Rock, 16.7% Scissors → has Scissors, so not possible.  
- **N (Paper > Scissors)**: 50% Paper, 33.3% Scissors, 16.7% Rock → has Scissors, so not possible.  
- **K (Rock > Paper)**: 50% Rock, 33.3% Paper, 16.7% Scissors → has Scissors, so not possible.  

Actually, looking at the list, only **E (Rock + Paper)** has 0% Scissors.  
But E’s distribution is 50% Rock, 50% Paper — not matching 32% Rock, 68% Paper.  

So maybe P1 is a **Markov player** reacting to P2’s constant Paper?  
Let’s check:

Markov players:  
- **X (Win-Last)**: Plays move that would have beaten opponent’s last move.  
If opponent always Paper, then Win-Last would always play Scissors after first move (since Scissors beats Paper). But P1 never plays Scissors → not X.  
- **Y (Lose-Last)**: Plays move that would have lost to opponent’s last move.  
If opponent always Paper, then Lose-Last would always play Rock after first move (since Rock loses to Paper). But P1 plays Rock only ~32% of time → not Y.  
- **Z (Copy-Last)**: Copies opponent’s last move.  
If opponent always Paper, then Z would always play Paper after first move. But P1 plays Paper ~68%, Rock ~32% → not Z either.

So P1 is not Markov.

Therefore, both are distribution players (since at most one Markov, and here none).  
P2 is C (Pure Paper).  
P1 must be a distribution player with **0% Scissors** and **Paper > Rock** in probability.  
The only distribution with 0% Scissors is E, but E is 50–50 Rock–Paper.  
So maybe P1 is **E** but with a biased random draw over 1000 rounds?  
32% Rock, 68% Paper in 1000 rounds is possible with E if randomness produces an uneven split — but 68% Paper is quite far from 50%. Possible but unlikely.

Alternatively, maybe P1 is **M (Paper > Rock)** but happened to draw Scissors 0 times in 1000 rounds?  
M has 16.7% Scissors, probability of 0 Scissors in 1000 rounds ≈ (5/6)^1000 ≈ extremely tiny → effectively impossible.

Given the data, the **most likely** is:  
P2 = C (Pure Paper)  
P1 = E (Rock + Paper) with an observed deviation due to randomness (possible, though large deviation).  
But 68% Paper vs 50% expected is a big shift — maybe P1 is actually **Paper-biased** but with Scissors probability ~0 observed by chance? That’s very improbable.

I: 25% Rock, 50% Paper, 25% Scissors.  
Observed: 32% Rock, 68% Paper, 0% Scissors.  
Scissors probability 25% but observed 0 in 1000 rounds → probability ≈ (0.75)^1000 ≈ impossibly small.
